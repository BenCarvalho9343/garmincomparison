Pros & Cons
The honest version.

Edge Explore 2

The tourer's companion
Pros
  • Biggest, clearest screen — easy to read at 45 kph
  • Full maps with automatic rerouting if you miss a turn
  • Touchscreen works intuitively — no menu diving
  • Points of interest: find a cafe mid-ride without your phone
  • Group LiveTrack so your mates know where you are
  • Varia radar and smart light compatible
  • Released 2022 — most modern hardware of the three
Cons
  • Bulkiest at 114 g — noticeable on a lightweight race bike
  • Touchscreen struggles with wet gloves or heavy rain
  • 16h battery — a big alpine day might cut it close
  • Training metrics are shallow — not for data-driven riders
  • No Di2/eTap integration, no NP or TSS

Edge 530

The serious rider's tool
Pros
  • Best-in-class training data — NP, TSS, IF, Training Load, VO₂ Max
  • 20-hour battery handles the longest sportives comfortably
  • Physical buttons work in any weather with any glove
  • Di2 / eTap electronic shifting integration
  • MTB dynamics for trail riders (jumps, grit, flow)
  • Full Connect IQ app ecosystem
  • Dual-band ANT+/Bluetooth for power meters
Cons
  • 2019 hardware — oldest of the three (still fully supported though)
  • Steep learning curve — overwhelming for casual riders
  • 2.6" screen is functional, not beautiful
  • Navigation works but it's not the reason you'd buy it
  • Button menu navigation feels slow compared to a touchscreen

Edge 130 Plus

The "just works" option
Pros
  • 33 g — lightest GPS computer you'll find, barely notice it
  • Cheapest by ~$100 compared to the other two
  • Simple enough that anyone can set it up in minutes
  • Still supports power meters, cadence, and HRM
  • Incident detection and LiveTrack included
  • ClimbPro on the Plus version
  • Makes a great race-day backup if your main unit dies
Cons
  • No maps — you will get lost without your phone
  • 1.8" screen limits how many data fields you can show
  • 12h battery — long days need a charge before you head out
  • No Wi-Fi sync — must keep your phone nearby
  • No training analytics beyond the basics
  • No Varia radar support

The Verdict
Which one should you actually buy?

Edge Explore 2

Best for →

Touring, bikepacking, commuting in unfamiliar areas, and any rider who wants to leave their phone in their pocket. If you've ever had to pull out your phone mid-ride to check a map, the Explore 2 solves that. It's also the right pick for less technical users who want a dead-simple touchscreen experience.

Edge 530

Best for →

Anyone training seriously — chasing segments, following a structured plan, racing, or riding with a power meter. The 530's analytics depth is genuinely useful if you know how to use it. Also the best pick for MTB riders who want jump and trail metrics. If you only buy one computer and want to grow into it, get the 530.

Edge 130 Plus

Best for →

Commuters, gym cyclists who want basic stats, or anyone put off by complexity. At 33 g it's unobtrusive enough for your lightest race bike and still pairs with a power meter and cadence sensor. Don't overthink it — it records your ride, shows your speed, and stays out of the way.

Bottom line: These three devices have more in common than they have differences — they all sync to Garmin Connect, support power meters, have ClimbPro and incident detection. The real split is navigation depth (Explore 2), training analytics (Edge 530), and simplicity/cost (Edge 130 Plus). The Explore 2 and 530 are the same price, so the choice between them is purely about what you ride for. If you spend winter analysing training load, get the 530. If you want to explore new roads without worrying about getting lost, get the Explore 2. If neither sounds like you, save $120 and get the 130.
